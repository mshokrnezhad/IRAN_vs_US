## Coding Assumptions
- `DiplomaticActivity` records mediated contacts, deadline extensions, U.N. moves, and public ceasefire frameworks, but it does not imply real de-escalation on its own.
- Iran's Hormuz posture was coded as a middle state between open passage and formal closure because the evidence repeatedly showed selective access, limited carveouts, and toll-like control.
- Hezbollah, Iraqi partner forces, and the Houthis were coded with lower military scores than the main state actors because they mattered structurally but were not the week's dominant operational drivers.
- Disputed or partly verified claims such as full battle-damage assertions were not coded as maximal observed success without corroboration.

## Figure List
### Escalation Intensity
- What it shows: Daily aggregate comparison between escalation pressure and diplomatic activity across the week.
- Main finding: Diplomacy became more visible after March 23, but escalation pressure stayed elevated because deadlines, reinforcements, strikes, and maritime coercion continued in parallel.

### Actor Behavior Heatmap
- What it shows: Actor-by-day intensity across military, diplomatic, proxy, infrastructure, and maritime categories.
- Main finding: Israel sustained the steadiest military pressure, Iran led maritime and infrastructure coercion, and the U.S. combined diplomacy with persistent coercive posture rather than wind-down behavior.

### Geographic Conflict Map
- What it shows: Theater-level concentration of each actor's primary weekly pressure.
- Main finding: The core map pattern is a Gulf Hormuz pressure center linked to Iranian and U.S. behavior, with Israel concentrated on Iran and Hezbollah anchored in Lebanon.

### Trigger-Response Flow
- What it shows: The clearest trigger-response loops selected from the highest-weight daily coding rows.
- Main finding: The week followed a visible escalation loop in which Israeli strike expansion, Iranian retaliation threats, U.S. deadline management, and Hormuz coercion kept feeding one another.

### Scenario Tree
- What it shows: Three forecast branches for the next seven days using the `MostLikely`, `EscalatoryAlternative`, and `DeEscalatoryAlternative` structure.
- Main finding: The baseline remains coercive endurance war, but the main upside and downside branches depend on whether Hormuz shifts toward coalition confrontation or broader carveouts.

### Forecast Risk Matrix
- What it shows: Concrete forecast objects plotted by probability and impact.
- Main finding: The most likely outcomes remain high-impact and persistent, while the most dangerous outcomes cluster around Hormuz confrontation and further Israeli target widening.

### Signals Dashboard
- What it shows: High-value triggers to monitor daily during the forecast window.
- Main finding: Deadline extensions, shipping-access changes, sensitive Israeli target expansion, and maritime-security mission planning are the best fast indicators of whether the baseline is holding.

## Evidence Limits
- The exact operational rules of Iran's selective-access system in Hormuz were only partly visible in the source reporting.
- Public claims about Iranian missile degradation remained less reliable than intelligence-based confirmation.
- Proxy activation timing outside Lebanon remained thinner and less decisive than the main U.S.-Iran-Israel evidence stream.
- These scores are comparative analytical judgments built from the reviewed briefs, not objective measurements of battlefield truth.
