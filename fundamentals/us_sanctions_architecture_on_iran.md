# U.S. Sanctions Architecture on Iran

## Purpose

This note explains how the U.S. sanctions system on Iran is structured, how it evolved, and what it is designed to do. It is written as a working analytical brief for research, not as a full legal treatise or a complete catalog of every sanctions authority, executive order, and designation.

## Core Pattern

U.S. sanctions on Iran have been defined by five recurring dynamics:

- long-term use of sanctions as a central policy instrument since 1979
- layering of many legal authorities rather than one single framework
- use of both primary and secondary sanctions to widen pressure
- expansion from hostage-related pressure to terrorism, proliferation, regional activity, and human rights
- periodic sanctions relief linked to diplomacy, followed by reimposition when diplomacy breaks down

## Why Secondary Sanctions Were a Turning Point

Early U.S. sanctions mostly governed what U.S. persons could do with Iran. Secondary sanctions changed the system by threatening penalties on non-U.S. actors doing business with Iran, especially in energy, finance, shipping, and proliferation-related sectors. That made Iran sanctions a global coercive architecture rather than only a bilateral embargo.

## Timeline

### 1979-1981: Emergency sanctions after the revolution and hostage crisis

The first major modern sanctions followed the Iranian Revolution and the seizure of the U.S. embassy in Tehran. Washington froze Iranian assets and began using economic restrictions as a coercive tool. These steps established the basic logic that economic pressure would be a durable part of U.S. policy toward the Islamic Republic.

### 1984-1991: Terrorism and proliferation concerns broaden the framework

As U.S. concerns expanded beyond the hostage crisis, sanctions policy widened to include Iran's support for militant groups and later its suspected proliferation activities. Iran's designation as a state sponsor of terrorism became a major legal and political anchor for later measures.

### 1992-1996: Congress creates the modern sanctions backbone

Congress began to codify Iran sanctions more aggressively in the 1990s. The Iran-Iraq Arms Nonproliferation Act targeted transfers linked to advanced weaponry, while the 1996 Iran and Libya Sanctions Act established a landmark secondary-sanctions model aimed at foreign investment in Iran's energy sector. This period is crucial because it moved sanctions policy from ad hoc pressure toward a durable statutory architecture.

### 2002-2013: Nuclear escalation drives financial and energy pressure

After the exposure of undeclared Iranian nuclear facilities in 2002, sanctions became increasingly tied to nuclear diplomacy. The United States, Congress, European partners, and the UN all increased pressure over time. U.S. measures expanded to target Iran's banking system, oil exports, shipping, insurance, and access to the dollar-based international financial system. By the early 2010s, sanctions were designed not only to punish Iran but to reduce the revenue available for the state.

### 2015-2016: JCPOA relief without full normalization

The Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action produced limited but significant sanctions relief tied to Iran's nuclear commitments. Nuclear-related sanctions were eased or waived in certain areas, but many sanctions linked to terrorism, missiles, human rights, and the IRGC remained in place. The architecture was not dismantled; it was partially suspended and selectively rewired.

### 2018-2020: "Maximum pressure" and reimposition

When the Trump administration withdrew from the JCPOA, the United States reimposed the sanctions that had been lifted or waived and added many more designations. The campaign aimed to cut Iranian oil revenue, isolate the financial sector, pressure shipping networks, and constrain the IRGC and its front companies. This restored the most coercive features of the architecture and signaled that sanctions relief would not be durable without a broader political settlement.

### 2021-2024: Enforcement, adaptation, and issue expansion

The Biden administration initially sought to revive the JCPOA, but the sanctions structure remained largely intact while diplomacy stalled. U.S. authorities continued to designate Iranian and third-country actors connected to oil sales, drones, missile procurement, human rights abuses, cyber activity, and support for Russia's war effort. Sanctions policy increasingly linked the nuclear file to wider regional and security concerns.

### 2024-present: Sanctions as a standing pressure system

Recent legislation and executive action have reinforced the role of sanctions as a standing instrument rather than a temporary measure. Measures tied to Iranian oil exports, drone and missile transfers, support for regional armed groups, and human rights abuses have continued to accumulate. By this stage, Iran sanctions function as a dense ecosystem of restrictions, licensing rules, waivers, designations, and sectoral deterrents.

## Main Components of the Architecture

### Primary sanctions

Primary sanctions govern what U.S. persons, U.S. companies, and transactions touching the United States can do. In the Iran case, they block most trade and financial dealings unless specifically authorized. This is the direct embargo layer of the system.

### Secondary sanctions

Secondary sanctions seek to deter non-U.S. persons from dealing with targeted Iranian sectors or actors by threatening loss of U.S. market access, financial access, or other penalties. These measures are one reason Iran sanctions have extraterritorial force well beyond the United States.

### Designations and blocking

OFAC designations freeze property and interests in property subject to U.S. jurisdiction and prohibit transactions with listed persons. In practice, designations often target networks rather than only single entities, including shipping companies, front firms, brokers, facilitators, and procurement channels.

### Sector-based pressure

Major target areas have included oil and gas, petrochemicals, shipping, ports, banking, insurance, metals, manufacturing, construction, automotive activity, and procurement for missile or military programs. Different authorities apply to different sectors, which is why the architecture is cumulative rather than simple.

### Humanitarian exceptions and general licenses

Even at high-pressure moments, U.S. policy has maintained exemptions or authorizations for certain humanitarian trade, especially food, medicine, and medical devices. Treasury also uses general licenses to permit specific otherwise prohibited activity, such as certain communications or internet-enabling tools. These carve-outs matter analytically because they show that the sanctions system is restrictive but not absolute.

## Main Phases at a Glance

### Phase 1: Revolutionary and terrorism sanctions, 1979-1991

Sanctions began as a response to the hostage crisis and later expanded around terrorism and national security concerns.

### Phase 2: Statutory expansion and secondary pressure, 1992-2012

Congress built the long-term legal backbone, and sanctions moved from bilateral restrictions toward broader coercion of foreign firms and financial channels.

### Phase 3: Nuclear relief and snapback logic, 2013-2018

The JCPOA showed that sanctions could be partially lifted for diplomatic reasons, but most of the architecture remained available for reactivation.

### Phase 4: Maximum pressure and system persistence, 2018-present

The post-JCPOA period restored and expanded sanctions as a standing strategy aimed at oil revenue, financial isolation, proliferation control, and pressure on Iran's regional behavior.

## Analytical Takeaways

- U.S. sanctions on Iran are best understood as an architecture, not a single program.
- Congress and the executive branch have both shaped the system, which is why it is legally layered and difficult to unwind quickly.
- Secondary sanctions are central because they globalize U.S. pressure beyond purely American actors.
- Sanctions relief has usually been partial and reversible, not full normalization.
- Humanitarian exemptions exist, but the practical effect of financial risk can still narrow real-world access and trade.
