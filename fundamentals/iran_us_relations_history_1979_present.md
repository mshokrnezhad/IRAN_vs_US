# Iran-U.S. Relations Since the 1979 Revolution

## Purpose

This note summarizes the major phases in relations between the Islamic Republic of Iran and the United States from the 1979 Iranian Revolution to the present period of heightened regional confrontation. It is written as a working analytical brief for research, not as an exhaustive diplomatic history.

## Core Pattern

Since 1979, Iran-U.S. relations have been defined by five recurring dynamics:

- ideological hostility after the revolution
- mutual deterrence and proxy conflict rather than normal diplomacy
- sanctions and economic coercion as a primary U.S. tool
- the nuclear issue as the central diplomatic file
- periodic military crises that stop short of full-scale war

## Why 1979 Was a Turning Point

The Iranian Revolution replaced the pro-U.S. monarchy of Mohammad Reza Shah Pahlavi with an Islamic Republic led by Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini. For the new regime, the United States represented foreign domination, especially because of long Iranian resentment over the 1953 coup against Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh. For Washington, revolutionary Iran quickly became a hostile power challenging U.S. influence in the Gulf and threatening American citizens and partners.

## Timeline

### 1979: Revolution and strategic rupture

The shah fell in early 1979, Khomeini returned from exile, and Iran was transformed from a close U.S. partner into an anti-American revolutionary state. The new leadership framed the United States as the "Great Satan" and treated resistance to U.S. influence as a core part of regime identity.

### 1979-1981: Hostage crisis and break in diplomatic relations

In November 1979, Iranian students seized the U.S. embassy in Tehran and took American diplomats hostage. The crisis lasted 444 days and became the defining trauma in modern Iran-U.S. relations. In April 1980, the United States severed diplomatic relations with Iran. Those ties have never been restored. Switzerland has since acted as the protecting power for U.S. interests in Iran.

### 1980-1988: Iran-Iraq War and deeper hostility

Iraq invaded Iran in 1980. During the war, the United States tilted toward Iraq, providing support even as Iraq used chemical weapons against Iranian forces. This period deepened Iran's belief that Washington sought to weaken or destroy the Islamic Republic. The war also pushed Iran toward long-term security self-reliance, missile development, and the use of regional non-state partners.

### 1983-1984: Terrorism designation and Lebanon

The 1983 Beirut barracks bombing, carried out by militants linked to Hezbollah, intensified U.S. accusations that Iran backed anti-American violence through proxies. In 1984, the United States designated Iran a state sponsor of terrorism. This became a durable pillar of U.S. policy and rhetoric.

### 1985-1987: Iran-Contra contradiction

Despite public hostility and an arms embargo, members of the Reagan administration secretly sold weapons to Iran in the Iran-Contra affair, partly to seek leverage over hostage releases in Lebanon. The episode showed that even at moments of maximum hostility, covert bargaining was still possible.

### 1987-1988: Tanker war, direct clashes, and Flight 655

As the Iran-Iraq War spilled into Gulf shipping, the United States increased naval involvement. In April 1988, U.S. forces attacked Iranian targets in Operation Praying Mantis after a U.S. warship was damaged by a mine. In July 1988, the USS Vincennes shot down Iran Air Flight 655, killing 290 civilians after mistaking it for a military aircraft. In Iran, this became one of the central symbols of U.S. hostility and impunity.

### 1990s: Containment and sanctions

After the Cold War, Washington moved toward "dual containment" of both Iraq and Iran. The 1990s saw expanding U.S. sanctions targeting Iran's energy sector, military procurement, and access to international commerce. The relationship was hostile, but the conflict remained mostly indirect.

### 1998-2001: Limited thaw

Under President Mohammad Khatami in Iran and the Clinton administration in the United States, there were modest signs of détente. Secretary of State Madeleine Albright publicly acknowledged the U.S. role in the 1953 coup. After the 9/11 attacks, Iran and the United States briefly cooperated against the Taliban and worked in parallel on post-Taliban arrangements in Afghanistan.

### 2002: "Axis of evil"

That limited opening collapsed when President George W. Bush labeled Iran part of an "axis of evil" together with Iraq and North Korea. In Tehran, the speech reinforced the view that Washington ultimately sought regime change, not coexistence.

### 2003: Iraq War reshapes the balance

The U.S. invasion of Iraq removed Saddam Hussein, Iran's main regional enemy, but also opened a new arena of Iran-U.S. competition. Iran expanded influence in Iraq through political allies and armed groups, while the United States accused Tehran of aiding militias that attacked U.S. forces. The Iraq War unintentionally increased Iran's regional reach.

### Mid-2000s to early 2010s: Nuclear issue becomes central

As concern over Iran's nuclear program grew, the dispute became the main channel through which relations were managed. The United States pursued sanctions, UN action, and pressure campaigns, while Iran insisted its program was peaceful and sovereign. The nuclear file became the core test of whether strategic de-escalation was possible.

### 2013-2015: Direct diplomacy and the JCPOA

The election of President Hassan Rouhani opened the way for sustained diplomacy. In 2013, Barack Obama and Rouhani had the highest-level direct contact since 1979. In 2015, Iran, the United States, and other major powers concluded the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action, or JCPOA. Iran accepted strict limits and monitoring on its nuclear program in exchange for sanctions relief. This was the most important diplomatic breakthrough in the relationship since the revolution.

### 2018: U.S. withdrawal from the JCPOA

President Donald Trump withdrew the United States from the nuclear deal and launched a "maximum pressure" sanctions campaign. Iran gradually reduced compliance with the agreement and increased uranium enrichment. The collapse of the deal marked a major return to confrontation.

### 2019: Escalation in the Gulf

Tensions rose sharply in and around the Persian Gulf. Washington blamed Iran for attacks on tankers and other regional provocations. The United States designated the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, or IRGC, as a foreign terrorist organization. The risk of open war increased, though both sides still avoided a full conventional conflict.

### 2020: Killing of Qasem Soleimani

In January 2020, the United States killed Qasem Soleimani, commander of the IRGC Quds Force, in Baghdad. This was one of the most consequential direct U.S. actions against Iran since 1979. Iran retaliated with missile strikes on U.S. positions in Iraq. The two states came close to a major war but then stepped back from further immediate escalation.

### 2021-2022: Failed effort to restore the nuclear deal

The Biden administration entered indirect talks aimed at bringing both sides back into compliance with the JCPOA. Negotiations continued intermittently but stalled over sequencing, sanctions relief, verification, and changing political conditions in both countries. Iran advanced its enrichment program while the diplomatic window narrowed.

### 2022-2023: Protests, drones, prisoner swap, and regional pressure

The United States sanctioned Iranian entities involved in the crackdown on the Mahsa Amini protest movement. At the same time, Washington also remained focused on Iran's nuclear advances, missile capabilities, and support for Russia's war effort through drone transfers. In 2023, the two sides reached a limited prisoner-swap arrangement, showing that narrow transactional diplomacy was still possible even amid wider confrontation.

### 2023-2024: Gaza war and direct Iran-Israel confrontation

After the October 2023 Hamas attack on Israel and the war in Gaza, Iran's regional network became even more central to U.S. policy. Iran-backed actors, including Hezbollah and the Houthis, increased pressure on Israel and U.S. interests. In 2024, Iran and Israel exchanged direct attacks for the first time at large scale. The United States backed Israel militarily and politically, while also trying to prevent a wider regional war. This made Iran-U.S. relations more tightly linked to the Iran-Israel confrontation than at any earlier point.

## Main Phases at a Glance

### Phase 1: Revolutionary rupture, 1979-1988

The relationship moved from alliance to open enmity, defined by the hostage crisis, the Iran-Iraq War, proxy violence, sanctions, and limited direct military confrontation.

### Phase 2: Containment and indirect conflict, 1989-2012

There was no normalization, but the main pattern shifted toward sanctions, proxy competition, and periodic diplomatic probing.

### Phase 3: Nuclear diplomacy and breakdown, 2013-2020

The JCPOA briefly created a framework for managed coexistence, but the U.S. withdrawal and Soleimani strike restored acute confrontation.

### Phase 4: Fragmented diplomacy under regional escalation, 2021-present

Nuclear diplomacy weakened, proxy warfare intensified, and Iran-U.S. tensions became increasingly entangled with the Gaza war, Red Sea disruptions, Iraq-Syria militia attacks, and direct Iran-Israel exchanges.

## Analytical Takeaways

- Iran and the United States have not had normal diplomatic relations since 1980, but they have repeatedly mixed confrontation with tactical bargaining.
- The memory of the 1979 hostage crisis shapes U.S. political attitudes, while the memory of the 1953 coup, support for the shah, backing for Iraq, sanctions, and Flight 655 shape Iranian attitudes.
- The nuclear issue became the main arena where both sides tested whether limited coexistence was possible.
- Regional actors matter. Iraq, Lebanon, Syria, Israel, the Gulf monarchies, Hamas, Hezbollah, and the Houthis have all influenced the bilateral relationship.
- Since 2023, the U.S.-Iran relationship has become more directly connected to the Iran-Israel military confrontation, raising the risk that proxy conflict could slide into direct war.
