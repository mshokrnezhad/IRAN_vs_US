It has now been one full month since the brutal bombing of my beloved country, IRAN, by the United States and Israel 😭.

IRAN is an ancient civilization with thousands of years of history, culture, resilience, and dignity. Like many before it, this hard period will also pass. IRAN will remain ✊.

I live far from my country, and in days like these, distance becomes one of the hardest things to carry 😭. Watching events unfold from afar creates a deep feeling of helplessness. To make that helplessness a little smaller, I decided to use what I know best: agentic AI.

Over the past month, I have been building a structured, repeatable analysis workflow to better grasp the IRAN-US-Israel conflict. The project is not about hot takes or emotional reactions. It is about turning daily reporting into a disciplined pipeline: daily news collection, weekly pattern analysis, scenario-based forecasting, forecast review, lessons learned, and figure generation.

In practice, the workflow uses AI agents to:

- collect and structure daily war-related developments
- synthesize weekly behavioral patterns across the main actors
- separate observation from prediction
- produce probability-based forecasts for the following week
- visualize military, diplomatic, and maritime behavior in figures that can be reviewed and updated over time

The output of last week’s analysis is visible in the attached heatmaps.
The first three figures show a very clear pattern. Israel appears to be pursuing sustained destructive pressure on IRAN with little meaningful regard for diplomacy or maritime stability. IRAN, by contrast, seems to be dividing its effort between military operations and maritime leverage, especially in ways that preserve pressure while keeping multiple options open. The United States appears to be sending its strongest signals in the diplomatic domain, while still maintaining a coercive posture in the background.

The forecast figure for the coming week suggests three broad possibilities. The most likely path is continuation of the current pattern: an endurance conflict with ongoing pressure, strikes, and strategic signaling. A less likely but still serious path is major escalation. The least likely scenario is a movement toward negotiations, restraint, and measurable de-escalation.

Of course, this is not prophecy. It is structured forecasting under uncertainty. The goal is not to claim certainty, but to make patterns more visible, assumptions more explicit, and updates more disciplined.

A week ago I shared the first slice of this project: how I am using an agentic, repeatable pipeline to turn daily reporting on the Iran–U.S.–Israel war into patterns, explicit forecasts, and figures I can revisit instead of doom-scrolling in silence.

That post attached the same figure set I am using again here: weekly actor heatmaps and geographic views, plus the scenario forecast panel for the period ahead.

**Quick look back (forecast window 2026-03-28 → 2026-04-03).**  
The central story in the model was an endurance conflict: strikes and Gulf exposure continuing alongside noisy diplomacy, without a clean de-escalation or a full Hormuz reopening. That baseline mostly matched what the briefs then showed—including the U.K.-led Hormuz contact group without Washington, the Iran–Oman transit story that moved markets briefly, and the widening target set inside Iran and across the Gulf. The framework underplayed how fast the Houthi axis would show up as a visible second pressure layer; that is now folded into the lessons learned for the next pass.

**Latest prediction (2026-04-04 → 2026-04-10).**  
With the F-15 episode, bridge and refinery/desalination strikes, and the Hormuz deadline rhetoric already in the tape, the updated forecast still puts most weight on **attritional continuity** (~48%): high-tempo air–missile–maritime fighting, Hormuz in a **restricted middle state** (signals and drafts, not normal tanker economics), and diplomacy running **parallel** to violence rather than replacing it. A substantial **escalatory** branch (~37%) is reserved for threshold crossings: sequenced infrastructure campaigns, more air losses or CSAR friction, larger GCC energy or water hits, or IRGC threats against named Western assets turning into verified incidents. A **de-escalatory** path (~15%) stays on the sheet but needs behavioral proof: sustained mutual quiet, published transit rules insurers believe, or a pause both sides acknowledge in the same text—not only headlines.

None of this is certainty. It is a disciplined way to keep assumptions visible and to update when the world refuses to cooperate with last week’s story.

Six weeks of tracking the Iran–U.S.–Israel conflict with agentic AI. The talks in Islamabad are now live — and this is what the pattern analysis predicts will come out of them.

**Most likely (~55–65%): a limited, face-saving deal.**
Both sides pause enough to reduce immediate pressure. Hormuz partially reopens under some monitored arrangement. Iran gets something transactional — sanctions relief or asset access. The nuclear file is deferred, not resolved. Lebanon keeps burning in the background.

**What comes after:** tense bargaining, disputes over verification, and a renewed escalation risk the next time one incident tips the balance.

The structural reality is simple: Iran still holds Hormuz and its nuclear program. The U.S. still holds economic leverage and strike capacity. Neither side can fully win, so neither side will fully concede.

Most probable outcome: **a ceasefire that holds until the next crisis.**
