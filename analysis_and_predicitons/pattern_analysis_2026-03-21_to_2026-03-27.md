## Executive Summary
- The week remained an endurance war, but its dominant pattern shifted from coalition-solicitation and mixed U.S. messaging to coercive bargaining under battlefield continuity: threats, deadline extensions, and mediated contacts multiplied without producing real behavioral de-escalation.
- Hormuz moved from ambiguous disruption toward a more structured controlled-access regime. Tehran signaled selective passage, humanitarian carveouts, and effective toll-like leverage rather than a simple binary of fully open versus formally closed.
- The United States repeatedly delayed threatened strikes on Iranian power plants while simultaneously reinforcing the theater, indicating a preference for leverage preservation over immediate threshold crossing.
- Israel stayed militarily aligned with Washington but increasingly acted outside the diplomatic track, culminating in wider target categories that included nuclear-linked and industrial sites.
- Iran paired public denial of negotiations with continued missile, drone, and Gulf-targeting pressure, showing that bargaining language did not replace coercion but sat on top of it.
- The Lebanon front stayed active and costly, but the week's main widening mechanisms were maritime coercion, direct state-to-state missile and air exchange, and expanding exposure of Gulf and U.S. facilities.
- The week also exposed a growing reality gap between public battlefield-victory rhetoric and intelligence-validated attrition, especially around Iran's remaining missile arsenal.
- Proxy actors remained important mainly as conditional escalation reserves or supporting pressure mechanisms rather than as the week's singular decisive drivers.

## Weekly Baseline
The week's overall state was coercive endurance war with managed bargaining theater. Relative to the previous week, the central change was that diplomacy became more visible but not more effective. Washington repeatedly invoked productive contacts, extensions, and proposals, yet reinforcement flows, Israeli strike tempo, Iranian retaliation, and maritime pressure all continued. The operational baseline therefore did not become de-escalation. Instead, it became a war in which battlefield action, deadline diplomacy, and economic choke-point management were happening simultaneously. The system looked less like a conflict moving toward ceasefire and more like an attritional contest in which both sides were testing whether pressure plus mediated contact could yield concessions without first reducing violence.

## Actor Behavior Analysis
### United States
- Objectives observed:
  - Preserve pressure on Iran while avoiding the most globally disruptive immediate escalation step of striking Iranian power plants.
  - Keep Hormuz usable enough to prevent full energy shock while denying Tehran uncontested control of the choke point.
  - Support Israeli operations while retaining U.S. freedom to define the timing and political framing of any negotiating track.
  - Demonstrate that U.S. goals can be met without immediate ground-force escalation.
- Actions taken:
  - Repeatedly threatened strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure, then extended deadlines on March 23 and March 26 instead of executing them.
  - Continued visible reinforcement through 82nd Airborne troops, Marines, amphibious assets, and broader theater-positioning.
  - Publicly advanced a 15-point ceasefire or action framework through intermediaries while senior officials kept coercive language active.
  - Used public messaging to separate talks, contacts, and deadline extensions from any commitment to real de-escalation.
  - Framed postwar Hormuz management as requiring broader international planning.
- Constraints:
  - Gulf exposure, shipping risk, and oil-market sensitivity made immediate maximal escalation costly.
  - Iranian threats and actual attacks on U.S. personnel and Gulf infrastructure raised the price of delay but also the risk of overreaction.
  - Diplomacy remained indirect and deniable, limiting Washington's ability to translate messaging into visible agreements.
  - Intelligence-based uncertainty about real Iranian missile degradation complicated triumphalist public claims.
- Pattern assessment:
  - The clearest U.S. pattern was coercive diplomacy backed by reinforcement. Behavior kept war pressure high while repeatedly postponing the most escalatory infrastructure strike.
  - A second pattern was deadline management rather than deadline execution. Extensions looked like bargaining tools, not de-escalation evidence.
  - A third pattern was operational seriousness under rhetorical flexibility: U.S. messaging oscillated, but reinforcements and planning remained consistent.

### Israel
- Objectives observed:
  - Continue degrading Iranian military, missile, and nuclear-linked capabilities.
  - Maintain pressure on Hezbollah and keep Lebanon tied to the main conflict system.
  - Preserve operational autonomy even while Washington tested mediation channels.
  - Expand the target set faster than diplomacy could lock in restraints.
- Actions taken:
  - Kept up strike waves in Tehran and other Iranian sites across the week.
  - Continued attacks in Beirut and southern Lebanon, including a larger southern-Lebanon military posture.
  - Publicly signaled that Israel was not part of the reported talks and would keep striking alongside the U.S.
  - Widened target categories by March 27 to heavy-water and yellowcake facilities and other industrially sensitive sites.
- Constraints:
  - Continued Iranian missile and drone fire, including strikes near sensitive Israeli facilities.
  - Dependence on U.S. military backing and U.S. management of regional blowback remained intact.
  - The Lebanon front continued imposing military and political costs.
  - Some economically explosive target classes, especially energy infrastructure, still drew visible U.S. concern.
- Pattern assessment:
  - Israel showed a repeated pattern of high operational tempo plus strategic broadening.
  - The week's most important Israeli shift was not tempo change but category change: target widening outran the diplomatic calendar.
  - This fits the structural pattern in which Israeli leaders use active wartime momentum to degrade Iran's future leverage, not only to answer immediate attacks.

### Iran
- Objectives observed:
  - Preserve deterrence credibility and regime continuity under sustained U.S.-Israeli attack.
  - Use Hormuz and wider Gulf vulnerability as cost-imposition tools against the U.S., Israel, and exposed partners.
  - Avoid public capitulation or acknowledgement of direct talks under fire.
  - Prove that battlefield punishment had not broken Iranian strike capacity or command continuity.
- Actions taken:
  - Rejected or denied direct negotiations even while counterproposals, messages, and mediated channels remained active.
  - Paired threats to power, desalination, IT, Gulf energy, and U.S.-linked infrastructure with selective-access claims for non-enemy shipping.
  - Maintained missile and drone pressure across Israel, Gulf states, and U.S.-exposed regional facilities, including the Prince Sultan Air Base strike.
  - Used legal-diplomatic channels, U.N. complaints, and humanitarian carveout language while still sustaining coercion.
  - Threatened heavy retaliation after Israeli strikes on nuclear-linked and industrial sites.
- Constraints:
  - U.S. and Israeli airpower continued to impose attritional costs on Iranian infrastructure and commanders.
  - A fully formal total closure of Hormuz still risked broader outside intervention.
  - Tehran's public victory rhetoric outran some independently validated battlefield data.
  - The regime still had to balance coercion with avoiding a clearly unified outside maritime coalition.
- Pattern assessment:
  - Iran's dominant pattern was controlled coercion, not clean closure. Tehran increasingly treated Hormuz as a permission-and-denial system with selective access, carveouts, and signaling value.
  - A second pattern was bargaining through denial: Tehran kept message traffic alive while insisting publicly that no real talks existed.
  - A third pattern was endurance-through-capacity signaling. Iranian behavior aimed to prove that missiles, maritime leverage, and regime coherence remained intact despite heavy strikes.

### Hezbollah
- Role this week:
  - Hezbollah remained part of a live Lebanon front, but the reporting emphasized Israeli strikes, force posture, and cumulative Lebanese costs more than new Hezbollah-led battlefield innovation.
- Pattern assessment:
  - The front stayed active enough to pin Israeli attention and sustain multi-front pressure.
  - The most accurate label remains active-bounded rather than full-surge.
  - Hezbollah's strategic value remained high, but the week did not show it replacing Hormuz or direct Iran-U.S.-Israel exchange as the main mechanism of pressure.

### Iraqi Partner Forces
- Role this week:
  - Iraqi militias were not the main observable mechanism in the March 21 to March 27 window, though Iraqi spillover and the broader risk to U.S. positions remained relevant in continuity.
- Pattern assessment:
  - The week reinforced Iraq as a latent escalation reserve rather than a lead front.
  - This matters analytically because widening pressure on the U.S. occurred without Iraq becoming the main delivery system.

### Ansar Allah / Houthis
- Role this week:
  - The Houthis were not the dominant operational actor in the main daily evidence, but by March 27 they were more clearly signaling conditions for entry.
- Pattern assessment:
  - Their current operational role was secondary to the Hormuz-centered struggle.
  - Their latent entry conditions became more visible, which raises their forecasting importance even though attribution for the week's main maritime pressure should remain conditional.

## Cross-Actor Interaction Patterns
- U.S. deadline extensions and public talk claims did not interrupt Israeli operational broadening; instead, the diplomatic track and military track ran in parallel with only partial restraint effects.
- Iranian selective-access behavior in Hormuz shaped U.S. choices directly by creating a middle state that was painful enough to demand response but ambiguous enough to complicate coalition escalation.
- Israeli target expansion increased Iranian incentive to threaten heavier retaliation and kept diplomacy from becoming behaviorally meaningful.
- Repeated attacks or alerts across Gulf states, plus the Saudi-base hit on U.S. troops, tightened the loop between maritime coercion and direct U.S. exposure.
- Limited humanitarian or agricultural carveouts through Hormuz showed that Tehran could combine coercion with narrow exceptions, reinforcing that bargaining and escalation were happening inside the same system rather than in separate phases.
