## Executive Summary
- The forecast's baseline was broadly right: the week stayed in sustained U.S.-Israeli military pressure, maritime disruption remained central, diplomacy stayed weak, and no formal Hormuz closure occurred.
- U.S. operational continuity, maritime-security emphasis, and energy-market management were among the strongest calls in the forecast.
- The forecast also read Iran correctly on two key points: Tehran kept using coercive ambiguity around Hormuz and paired wartime pressure with visible regime-resilience messaging rather than obvious collapse behavior.
- Israel's continued high-tempo strikes, Lebanon pressure, and regime-delegitimization rhetoric were all forecast well.
- Hezbollah and Iraqi partner-force calls were mostly accurate, though the evidence for Hezbollah's own independent operational tempo was thinner than the forecast's confident framing implied.
- The biggest misses were not the baseline scenario but secondary political mechanisms: the forecast did not anticipate how visible the U.S.-Israel war-aim gap would become or how much internal U.S. legitimacy fracture would enter the week's story.
- The Houthi-specific call was weaker than the broader maritime-risk call; shipping danger stayed high, but the reviewed week did not clearly show the Houthis as the main observable mechanism.
- Overall, this was a solid behavioral forecast with underweighted alliance-management and domestic-political complications.

## Actor-By-Actor Review
### United States
- What was forecast: Continued strikes, stronger maritime-security posture, leadership pressure, and energy-market management without a ground invasion
- What happened: The U.S. sustained military operations, sought a Hormuz coalition, sent more forces, pushed allied blacklisting of the IRGC and Hezbollah, and used oil waivers or pauses to ease price pressure
- What was missed: The forecast underweighted how visible the contradiction between reinforcement and public wind-down talk would become, and it did not anticipate the Joe Kent resignation-driven legitimacy fight

### Israel
- What was forecast: High-tempo strikes in Iran, an active-expanding Lebanon front, and continued regime-delegitimization rhetoric
- What happened: Israel kept striking Iran, continued attacks in Lebanon, and pushed broad claims about Iran's degraded capabilities and future regional order
- What was missed: The forecast did not explicitly anticipate how publicly Washington would begin managing and constraining some Israeli target choices, especially around gas infrastructure

### Iran
- What was forecast: Hormuz coercive ambiguity, regime-resilience messaging, selective multi-front pressure, and continued economic coercion
- What happened: Iran kept the shipping crisis alive without a formal closure, threatened Gulf energy sites, rejected de-escalation, cracked down internally, and used Mojtaba's Nowruz message to reinforce continuity
- What was missed: The forecast did not explicitly model the jump from regional coercion to global tourism-threat rhetoric, which widened Tehran's signaling footprint even if not yet matched by action

### Hezbollah
- What was forecast: An active-expanding but not guaranteed full-surge front that would keep raising Lebanese costs
- What happened: Lebanon remained an active battlefield and Israel kept hitting Hezbollah-linked targets, with cumulative Lebanese casualties rising sharply
- What was missed: The week offered less direct reporting on Hezbollah's own daily operational tempo than on Israel's response, making the actor-specific confidence slightly too strong

### Hashd
- What was forecast: A latent or sporadic front rather than the dominant widening mechanism
- What happened: Iraq stayed a spillover risk, most clearly through the Baghdad embassy strike on 2026-03-14, but it did not become the central theater
- What was missed: Very little; this actor call was well calibrated for the reviewed window

### Ansar Allah / Houthis
- What was forecast: Supportive maritime disruption behavior that would help keep shipping risk elevated
- What happened: Maritime risk remained high, but the reviewed week did not clearly tie the main disruptive pattern to Houthi actions
- What was missed: The forecast should have separated maritime function from actor attribution more explicitly

## Missed Developments
- Public divergence between U.S. and Israeli war aims became a major observable issue, especially after Gabbard's 2026-03-19 testimony and Trump's request that Israel halt future gas-field attacks
- Internal U.S. legitimacy fracture entered the week in a visible way through Joe Kent's resignation and the public fight over whether Iran posed an imminent threat
- Iran expanded its threat rhetoric beyond the regional battlefield on 2026-03-20 by warning that tourism and recreational sites worldwide would not be safe for its enemies

## Why The Forecast Missed
- Over-weighted signals:
  - The assumption that battlefield and maritime dynamics would matter more than alliance-management frictions inside the same week
  - The assumption that maritime disruption could be assigned too neatly to the Houthi-support mechanism rather than to a mixed-attribution shipping-risk environment
- Under-weighted signals:
  - Evidence that U.S. and Israeli objectives were aligned in warfighting but not identical in target priorities or desired end-state
  - The possibility that internal U.S. debate over war justification would become a meaningful near-term development
  - Iran's incentive to broaden global psychological-threat messaging even without opening a new operational front
- Timing errors:
  - The forecast did not expect alliance-management issues and U.S. domestic legitimacy conflict to become so visible within the same seven-day window
- Actor-control errors:
  - The Houthi-linked maritime call was too actor-specific relative to the evidence actually available during the week
- Propaganda traps:
  - The forecast did not stress-test public claims of quick success or imminent winding down against the harder evidence of reinforcements, funding requests, and continued operations
- Structural misunderstandings:
  - The review shows that coalition politics and domestic legitimacy can become first-order forecasting variables during an endurance war, not just background commentary

## Calibration Notes
- Which confidence calls were too high:
  - The Houthi-specific maritime attribution call
  - The implied confidence that Hezbollah's own observable operational tempo would be clearly visible in daily reporting
- Which confidence calls were too low:
  - The baseline expectation that energy-market management would stay central to wartime behavior
  - The expectation that no real diplomatic breakthrough would occur
- Which actor was hardest to estimate correctly:
  - The United States, because its public rhetoric, operational reinforcement, alliance management, and domestic legitimacy signals pointed in different directions at once

## Improvements For Next Forecast
- Separate alliance alignment from alliance objective convergence; forecast where U.S. and Israeli priorities may diverge even if joint military pressure continues
- Track coalition solicitation and coalition commitment as different indicators; public calls for help at Hormuz are not the same as actual allied entry
- When maritime disruption remains high but attribution is mixed, forecast the operational function first and keep actor attribution explicitly conditional
- Add a domestic-legitimacy watchlist for the United States, especially when senior security officials, intelligence claims, and White House rhetoric start diverging publicly
- Weight deployments, budget requests, and target-selection changes more heavily than optimistic or contradictory public statements about winding down
