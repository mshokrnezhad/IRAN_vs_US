## Executive Summary

- The week stayed firmly inside a high-intensity endurance war, but the dominant pattern shifted from simple operational continuation to contradiction management: the United States expanded forces and planning even while talking about nearing objectives or winding down.
- Washington spent much of the week trying to internationalize or formalize pressure around Hormuz, but coalition solicitation repeatedly outpaced coalition formation, showing broad concern without broad operational commitment.
- Iran's most repeated behavioral pattern was energy-and-shipping coercion under ambiguity: threats to Gulf ports, refineries, and shipping continued, but Tehran still stopped short of a clearly declared total closure of Hormuz.
- U.S.-Israeli military alignment remained strong, yet public evidence of differing war aims became much clearer, especially around energy targets, desired end states, and the meaning of battlefield success.
- Israel maintained offensive tempo in Iran and Lebanon, but U.S. behavior showed that Washington was trying to cap at least one escalatory lane by discouraging repeated attacks on Iranian gas infrastructure.
- Iran paired external pressure with internal security tightening and regime-resilience messaging, reinforcing the pattern that heavy bombardment was producing harder internal control rather than visible near-term state collapse.
- The week elevated political variables that were underweighted before: domestic U.S. legitimacy disputes, allied reluctance, and war-aim divergence now mattered alongside missiles, drones, and shipping incidents.
- Proxy fronts remained relevant but secondary to the maritime-energy contest; Lebanon stayed active, Iraq remained a spillover risk, and Houthi-linked pressure remained strategically plausible even where attribution stayed mixed.

## Weekly Baseline

The week's overall state was sustained interstate war under a widening political-management burden. Relative to the previous week, the key change was that battlefield continuity no longer explained the whole system. The U.S. and Israel still retained the operational initiative, and Iran still relied on distributed retaliation, but the most important new signals came from how the war was being managed: Washington sought maritime help but failed to generate a visible coalition, tried to restrain some Israeli energy targeting while reinforcing the theater, and faced a more open domestic argument about the war's justification. Iran, for its part, sustained pressure on shipping, Gulf energy exposure, and public morale while signaling that leadership losses or hardship would not produce surrender. The baseline therefore looked like coercive endurance with growing friction inside the anti-Iran camp, not de-escalation.

## Actor Behavior Analysis

### United States

- Objectives observed:
  - Keep military pressure on Iran while preventing Tehran from successfully coercing oil flows and Gulf partners.
  - Build a broader legal, diplomatic, and possibly naval framework against Iran's core military and proxy architecture.
  - Support Israel while preserving U.S. control over the conflict's most economically dangerous escalatory lanes.
  - Sustain domestic and alliance backing long enough to continue the campaign.
- Actions taken:
  - Repeatedly urged outside countries to help secure Hormuz, but did not secure clear public commitments.
  - Pushed allies to blacklist the IRGC and Hezbollah, widening the campaign beyond battlefield action into institutional pressure.
  - Weighed reinforcements and possible new operational options, then visibly deployed additional Marines, sailors, and amphibious assets.
  - Managed energy pressure through waivers or pauses affecting already-loaded Iranian oil and wider emergency oil-release coordination.
  - Publicly and privately signaled restraint toward some Israeli energy targeting while continuing the broader war effort.
- Constraints:
  - Gulf partners remained exposed to missile, drone, and infrastructure threats.
  - Allied states were willing to consult but still hesitant to be drawn directly into the war.
  - Public U.S. messaging was internally contradictory, weakening clarity on actual war trajectory.
  - Internal legitimacy strain surfaced through Joe Kent's resignation and the public fight over imminent-threat claims.
- Pattern assessment:
  - The clearest U.S. pattern was operational escalation with rhetorical inconsistency. Behavior pointed to a longer, broader campaign even when official messaging flirted with closure.
  - A second pattern was coalition solicitation without coalition conversion. Washington repeatedly tried to broaden participation, but partner caution remained a real constraint.
  - A third pattern was selective escalation management: the U.S. was not trying to stop the war, but it was trying to shape how allies escalated inside it, especially around energy-market risk.

### Israel

- Objectives observed:
  - Sustain pressure on Iranian military, missile, and nuclear-linked capacity.
  - Keep Hezbollah under sustained pressure to prevent northern-front relief for Iran.
  - Convert military gains into broader strategic weakening of Iran's future regional position.
  - Preserve freedom of action even where U.S. priorities were not identical.
- Actions taken:
  - Continued wide strike waves against Tehran and other Iranian targets.
  - Sustained or intensified attacks on Hezbollah-linked targets in Lebanon and Beirut.
  - Publicly advanced sweeping claims about Iranian missile and enrichment degradation.
  - Accepted at least some visible U.S. signaling that repeated attacks on gas infrastructure were undesirable.
- Constraints:
  - Iranian missile and drone fire into Israel continued, including more complex cluster-missile use.
  - Israel remained strategically dependent on U.S. military backing and U.S. management of wider regional fallout.
  - The Lebanon front imposed ongoing costs without yet becoming clearly separable from the Iran theater.
  - Public divergence with Washington over end-state and target priorities became harder to hide.
- Pattern assessment:
  - Israel showed a repeated pattern of tempo preservation plus strategic broadening. It treated Iran, Lebanon, and postwar regional order as linked parts of one campaign.
  - The week's most important Israeli change was not reduced aggression, but clearer evidence that Israeli objectives extended beyond the narrower U.S. framing.
  - Israeli behavior still fit the long-term pattern of using military momentum to degrade future Iranian leverage, but with more visible U.S. guardrails on some target classes.

### Iran

- Objectives observed:
  - Preserve deterrence credibility and regime continuity despite sustained U.S.-Israeli strikes.
  - Raise the cost of war for the U.S., Israel, and Gulf states through shipping, energy, and regional spillover pressure.
  - Avoid visible capitulation or premature negotiation under fire.
  - Show that succession and leadership losses would not produce regime collapse.
- Actions taken:
  - Rejected ceasefire or de-escalation language repeatedly, including via Mojtaba Khamenei's harder line.
  - Threatened and in some cases helped drive disruption around Gulf ports, energy facilities, shipping lanes, and airports.
  - Kept missile and drone pressure on Israel while using new or more challenging strike forms such as cluster warheads.
  - Tightened internal security, arresting alleged informants and using wartime messaging to reinforce loyalty and resilience.
  - Broadened rhetorical threat space, eventually including warnings about global leisure and tourism targets.
- Constraints:
  - The U.S. and Israel retained air and naval superiority and kept pressure on Iranian infrastructure and leadership networks.
  - Tehran still faced the risk that a formal or fully enforced Hormuz closure would trigger wider outside entry.
  - External political support from other major powers did not materially change battlefield conditions.
  - Iranian public claims about battlefield outcomes were often partly propagandistic or unsupported.
- Pattern assessment:
  - Iran's dominant pattern was coercive ambiguity centered on energy and shipping. Tehran repeatedly signaled capacity to widen pain without always crossing the most escalatory formal threshold.
  - A second pattern was resilience-through-control: crackdown, defiance, and symbolic continuity remained core wartime behaviors.
  - The week also showed that Iran preferred layered pressure over synchronized maximum-force proxy activation. It kept many levers live without clearly maxing out all of them at once.

### Hezbollah

- Role this week:
  - Hezbollah remained part of an active Lebanon front, but the week's evidence was stronger on Israel's continued strikes against Hezbollah-linked targets than on detailed daily Hezbollah operational tempo.
- Pattern assessment:
  - The front stayed active enough to keep Israel committed in Lebanon and to increase Lebanese costs.
  - The best reading is active-bounded to active-expanding relevance, not clearly full-surge escalation.
  - This fits the structural pattern in which Hezbollah is Iran's most consequential partner, but not automatically operating at maximum intensity every week.

### Hashd

- Role this week:
  - Iraq remained a spillover theater, most visibly through the Baghdad embassy helipad strike on 2026-03-14, but no sustained Iraqi militia campaign became the main widening mechanism.
- Pattern assessment:
  - The key pattern was continued latent relevance. Iraqi groups remained a plausible escalation option against U.S. interests, but not the dominant observable tool in this window.
  - This reinforces the lesson that wider anti-U.S. pressure can intensify without Iraqi militias becoming the lead instrument.

### Ansar Allah / Houthis

- Role this week:
  - The week kept maritime risk elevated, but the reviewed reporting did not clearly show a Houthi-centered mechanism driving the main shipping and Gulf pressure story.
- Pattern assessment:
  - The strategic function associated with the Houthis remained highly relevant because maritime disruption stayed central to the conflict.
  - Attribution, however, remained mixed enough that the strongest analytic call is about function rather than confident actor assignment.

## Cross-Actor Interaction Patterns

- U.S. attempts to rally a maritime-security coalition interacted directly with Iranian coercive ambiguity: each new shipping or Gulf-energy threat increased pressure for outside involvement, but partner reluctance preserved Tehran's room to keep coercing below the threshold of a visibly united counter-coalition.
- Israeli offensive pressure and Iranian distributed retaliation sustained each other in a classic escalation loop, but the loop was now moderated at the margin by U.S. concern over energy-market blowback.
- The public surfacing of different U.S. and Israeli war aims created a new interaction pattern: battlefield alignment continued, but alliance-management became an active part of the conflict rather than background noise.
- Iranian threats to Gulf sites and continued disruption behavior pushed Washington into energy-market management and selective sanctions relief, showing that Tehran could influence U.S. economic policy even without forcing a strategic reversal.
- Internal U.S. dissent and alliance reluctance did not stop military operations, but they created a restraint loop by increasing the political cost of open-ended escalation.

## Indicators To Watch Next Week

- Whether U.S. reinforcements transition into visible escort, interdiction, or shoreline-control operations rather than remaining deterrent deployments
- Any actual public commitments by allied navies to Hormuz security missions
- Further U.S. efforts to steer or restrain Israeli target classes, especially around energy infrastructure
- Any verified direct U.S.-Iran maritime clash tied to shipping protection or mine-clearing
- Additional Iranian threats or attacks against Gulf energy facilities, airports, or major commercial nodes
- Whether Tehran continues globalized threat rhetoric or lets that line fade without operational follow-through
- New signs of Hezbollah moving from an active front into denser and more sustained salvos
- Any shift from isolated Iraqi spillover to a clearly repeated campaign against U.S. positions
- Further U.S. legal or diplomatic moves against the IRGC and Hezbollah that convert wartime alignment into standing institutional pressure
- Any mutually acknowledged mediator contact that coincides with narrower battlefield behavior rather than rhetoric alone

## Information Gaps

- The real readiness and rules of any prospective Hormuz coalition remained unclear throughout the week.
- U.S. contingency planning for shoreline, Kharg Island, or uranium-security missions was reported, but decision status and military feasibility remain uncertain.
- Several Iranian claims about UAE-linked U.S. launch sites, practical Hormuz openness, and wider target effects were not independently established.
- Israeli claims about the scale of Iranian nuclear and missile degradation were partly contested, including by the IAEA.
- The exact day-to-day operational role of Hezbollah, Iraqi militias, and Houthis remained harder to confirm than their strategic relevance.
- It remains uncertain how much domestic U.S. legitimacy friction will affect actual war duration inside the next forecast window.
