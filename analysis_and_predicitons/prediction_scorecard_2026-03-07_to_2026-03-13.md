## Executive Summary
- The core baseline was broadly right: the week stayed in a high-intensity U.S.-Israeli air and naval campaign, Iran kept retaliating, shipping disruption stayed central, and diplomacy remained weak.
- U.S. and Israeli operational-continuity calls were the strongest part of the forecast. There was no pause, no ground invasion, and no meaningful retreat from maximalist public rhetoric.
- The forecast also read the maritime dimension correctly, especially the expectation of elevated shipping risk short of a formal Hormuz closure.
- Hezbollah was directionally forecast correctly as active but not yet in full all-out war, but the week leaned more escalatory on the Lebanon front than the prediction implied.
- The biggest miss was underweighting how quickly succession politics would become the defining development. Mojtaba Khamenei's formal selection on 2026-03-08 materially changed the command picture.
- The forecast underpredicted how much the war's political economy would matter inside the week: record-scale strategic oil-release planning, Russian-oil easing, and direct energy-market management became part of the conflict response.
- The forecast was also too ready to treat Hashd as the most likely reserve escalation lever. In practice, the main widening mechanism remained maritime pressure, Gulf infrastructure attacks, and coercive ambiguity around Hormuz.
- Overall, the review points to a forecast that captured the war's operating logic but missed some of the week's most important mechanism shifts and political turning points.

## Actor-By-Actor Review
### United States
- What was forecast: Continued strikes, more shipping protection, maximalist rhetoric, and no ground invasion
- What happened: The U.S. sustained the campaign, escalated maritime protection around Hormuz, kept extreme rhetoric, and widened leadership pressure through reward notices and regime-framing
- What was missed: The review underpredicted how central direct energy-market management and public intelligence-policy tension would become

### Israel
- What was forecast: High-tempo strikes in Iran, continued retaliation in Lebanon, and broader strategic aims than immediate defense
- What happened: Israel maintained tempo, expanded Lebanon operations when Hezbollah fired, and sharpened regime-collapse and successor-targeting rhetoric
- What was missed: The forecast did not fully foreground how openly Israeli messaging would shift toward explicit regime-collapse conditions

### Iran
- What was forecast: Continued retaliation through drones, missiles at reduced tempo, maritime pressure, public defiance, and selective proxy use
- What happened: Iran kept using shipping, oil, infrastructure, and drone pressure while publicly rejecting ceasefire logic; Mojtaba's selection hardened the command narrative
- What was missed: Rapid succession resolution and the importance of regime-resilience signaling were underweighted

### Hezbollah
- What was forecast: Continued but bounded attacks that keep the northern front active without guaranteeing all-out war
- What happened: Hezbollah remained active and triggered heavier Israeli responses, especially by 2026-03-12
- What was missed: The week ran somewhat hotter on the Lebanon front than the forecast's baseline implied

### Hashd
- What was forecast: Mostly latent reserve escalation potential, with limited activation more likely than dominant-front entry
- What happened: Iraq mattered as spillover terrain, but no clearly confirmed Iraqi partner-force campaign became the main widening instrument
- What was missed: The forecast overweighted Iraqi militias relative to maritime and Gulf mechanisms of escalation

### Ansar Allah / Houthis
- What was forecast: Continued or supportive maritime disruption behavior raising shipping risk
- What happened: Shipping disruption intensified and attribution stayed mixed in several incidents
- What was missed: The exact actor was often unclear, but the forecast's structural call about maritime disruption was still largely borne out

## Missed Developments
- Mojtaba Khamenei's rapid formal selection as supreme leader turned succession from background uncertainty into the central political fact of the week
- Oil-market stabilization became an immediate policy front through record reserve-release planning and temporary easing of some Russian oil restrictions
- Regime-resilience signaling became a major analytical issue after U.S. intelligence judged Iran stable and senior officials appeared publicly in Tehran

## Why The Forecast Missed
- Over-weighted signals:
  - The prior week's logic that Hashd were the most plausible reserve escalation lever
  - The idea that coalition burden would mainly show up as Gulf-state pressure on Washington rather than fast-moving energy-market intervention
- Under-weighted signals:
  - The speed with which Iran's succession process could be resolved under wartime pressure
  - The possibility that regime-resilience evidence would emerge quickly and directly contradict collapse narratives
  - The degree to which global oil-system management would become part of the same week's conflict response
- Timing errors:
  - The forecast treated succession uncertainty as persistent, when it resolved almost immediately
  - It also slightly lagged the pace at which the Lebanon front moved from bounded pressure toward expansion risk
- Actor-control errors:
  - Hashd were treated as too central to Tehran's widening toolkit
  - Hezbollah was read as somewhat more tightly bounded than the week justified
- Propaganda traps:
  - The forecast did not explicitly stress-test U.S. victory and regime-collapse rhetoric against institutional durability indicators
- Structural misunderstandings:
  - The forecast captured the military structure of the war better than the leadership-transition and energy-governance structure unfolding inside the same week

## Calibration Notes
- Which confidence calls were too high:
  - The implicit weighting behind Hashd as the most plausible rapid widening lever
  - The practical "boundedness" implied in the Hezbollah baseline
- Which confidence calls were too low:
  - The expectation that shipping disruption would remain central was stronger than the medium-confidence language suggested
  - The expectation that U.S. leadership rhetoric would stay maximalist also proved stronger than a medium-confidence label implied
- Which actor was hardest to estimate correctly:
  - Iran, because battlefield attrition, succession politics, public messaging, and coercive ambiguity all changed at once

## Improvements For Next Forecast
- Add an explicit succession-path section whenever wartime leadership uncertainty exists, including a "rapid resolution" branch rather than treating succession only as fog
- Separate "where pressure widens" from "which actor widens it" so mechanism errors are easier to avoid
- Include a dedicated regime-resilience test that weighs intelligence, public appearances, internal repression, and command continuity against collapse rhetoric
- Treat oil-market management and emergency energy policy as forecastable conflict behavior, not just downstream background effects
- Use a tighter scale for proxy-front forecasts: latent, active-bounded, active-expanding, and full-surge
- Require every strong public claim about imminent victory or collapse to be checked against observable institutional indicators before it shapes scenario weighting
