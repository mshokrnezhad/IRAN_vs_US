# Prediction - 2026-03-28 to 2026-04-03

## Scope

- Forecast window: 2026-03-28 to 2026-04-03
- Analysis basis: pattern analysis from the prior 7 days

## Executive Summary

- The most likely outcome is continued coercive endurance war in which mediated contact, deadline extensions, and public talk claims continue, but observable strike and maritime pressure do not narrow in a durable way.
- Hormuz is most likely to remain in a middle state of selective controlled passage: limited carveouts, politically filtered access, and continued global shipping risk rather than clean reopening or formal all-stop closure.
- The United States is more likely to keep reinforcing and bargaining than to immediately execute its threatened strike on Iranian power plants, unless U.S. casualties or shipping disruption worsen sharply.
- Israel is likely to keep widening sensitive target classes faster than diplomacy can constrain them, especially because it has stayed publicly outside the negotiating track.
- Iran is likely to keep negotiations publicly conditional or denied while continuing missile, drone, Gulf-pressure, and maritime coercion meant to prove it still holds escalation cards.
- Intelligence-validated damage assessments should be weighted more heavily than public victory rhetoric; Iran's remaining missile capacity is still likely sufficient to sustain meaningful retaliation during this forecast window.
- Hezbollah is likely to stay active-bounded in Lebanon, while Iraqi partner forces and the Houthis remain more plausible conditional escalators than baseline lead fronts.
- The biggest risk to this baseline is a threshold-crossing event such as a larger direct hit on U.S. forces, a visible allied Hormuz mission, or a new Israeli strike set against especially sensitive nuclear, leadership, or energy-linked targets.

## Cross-Theater Forecast

- Maritime:
  - Hormuz is likely to remain neither fully open nor fully shut.
  - The most likely pattern is selective passage, controlled exceptions, shipping intimidation, and external planning for a future security regime.
- Air and missile campaign:
  - Israeli strike tempo is likely to remain high.
  - Iran is likely to preserve enough missile and drone capability to keep retaliatory salvos and U.S.-partner exposure credible.
- Proxy front activation:
  - Lebanon is likely to remain the most active partner front.
  - Iraq and the Houthis are more likely to remain conditional escalators than immediate lead mechanisms unless another threshold is crossed.
- Diplomacy and mediation:
  - Public claims of contacts or productive discussions are likely to continue.
  - The better test of real de-escalation will be reduced targeting and broader shipping access, not rhetoric alone.
- Economic and sanctions signaling:
  - Market-management logic is likely to remain active in Washington.
  - More coercive legal or diplomatic framing around postwar shipping access may appear even without a fresh major sanctions package.

## Key Triggers To Watch

- Trigger: Another direct strike causing significant U.S. casualties at a regional base or naval asset
- Why it matters: This would increase pressure on Washington to stop extending deadlines and escalate directly
- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

- Trigger: Public announcement of an allied Hormuz escort, interdiction, or mine-clearance mission
- Why it matters: This would mark coalition formation finally catching up to consultation
- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

- Trigger: Israel conducts further strikes on highly sensitive nuclear-linked, leadership, or critical industrial targets
- Why it matters: This would strengthen the pattern that target widening is outrunning diplomacy
- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

- Trigger: Iran widens Hormuz access beyond humanitarian and agricultural cargo into broader commercial passage
- Why it matters: This would be one of the strongest observable signs that bargaining is affecting behavior
- Which scenario it supports: De-escalatory / negotiation alternative

- Trigger: Mutually acknowledged talks coincide with several quieter days in missile salvos, Gulf alerts, and Israeli strike breadth
- Why it matters: This would distinguish real behavioral de-escalation from another rhetorical pause
- Which scenario it supports: De-escalatory / negotiation alternative

- Trigger: Intelligence-based reporting shows a much sharper decline in Iran's usable missile inventory than currently confirmed
- Why it matters: This would weaken the baseline expectation of sustained Iranian retaliatory capacity
- Which scenario it supports: Most likely or de-escalatory, depending on concurrent diplomacy

## Fast-Fail Signals

- The forecast is wrong quickly if the U.S. launches prompt strikes on Iranian power plants or enters a visible site-seizure or shoreline-control operation early in the window.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if a formal ceasefire or openly acknowledged direct U.S.-Iran negotiation framework appears and is matched by narrower military behavior.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if Hormuz shifts abruptly out of the middle state into either broad normal commercial reopening or openly enforced all-traffic closure.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if Hezbollah, Iraqi partner forces, and maritime actors all surge together into a clearly synchronized multi-front expansion.

## Confidence And Limits

- The biggest uncertainties are the real U.S. threshold for acting on its threats, the speed of Israeli target-category expansion, and the durability of Iran's selective-control model in Hormuz.
- Evidence is thinner on exact proxy activation timing than on the core U.S.-Iran-Israel and maritime patterns.
- Public battlefield claims remain noisier than intelligence-validated evidence, especially on Iranian missile attrition.
- Confidence labels describe evidence strength for specific behavior forecasts; scenario probabilities reflect relative path likelihood, not certainty.
