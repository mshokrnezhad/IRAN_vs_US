# Prediction - 2026-03-14 to 2026-03-20

## Scope
- Forecast window: 2026-03-14 to 2026-03-20
- Analysis basis: pattern analysis from the prior 7 days

## Executive Summary
- The most likely outcome is continued high-intensity coercive endurance warfare: U.S.-Israeli strikes remain active, Iran keeps pressuring shipping and Gulf-linked targets, and no meaningful negotiation breakthrough appears.
- The central near-term contest is likely to stay concentrated on Hormuz, merchant shipping, energy-market stabilization, and leadership pressure rather than on a single decisive battlefield breakthrough inside Iran.
- Mojtaba Khamenei's succession is likely to produce further hardline messaging and visible regime-resilience theater, but not necessarily an immediate outright closure of Hormuz or synchronized maximum-force proxy activation.
- The United States is likely to keep combining military action with maritime protection, intelligence-financial targeting of senior Iranian figures, and emergency oil-market management.
- Israel is likely to maintain offensive tempo in Iran and keep the Lebanon front in an active-expanding state if Hezbollah continues firing.
- Hezbollah is more likely to remain active-expanding than to fall quiet, but a full-surge regional proxy war is still a secondary rather than baseline scenario.
- The biggest escalatory risks are a direct U.S.-Iran maritime clash, a declared or de facto Hormuz closure attempt, or a clear transition from sporadic proxy involvement to synchronized multi-front activation.
- The biggest uncertainty is not whether pressure continues, but which mechanism dominates next: maritime coercion, Lebanon expansion, leadership targeting, or a limited mediated narrowing of attacks under economic strain.

## Cross-Theater Forecast
- Maritime:
  - Shipping risk in and around Hormuz is likely to remain elevated.
  - The most likely pattern is continued disruption, mining pressure, and coercive ambiguity rather than an immediate fully declared permanent closure.
- Air and missile campaign:
  - U.S.-Israeli strikes are likely to continue at high tempo.
  - Iran is more likely to rely on a mixed set of drones, limited missiles, maritime threats, and distributed pressure than on a return to early-war mass salvos alone.
- Proxy front activation:
  - Lebanon is likely to remain the most active-expanding partner front.
  - Iraq remains more likely to produce sporadic or opportunistic incidents than to become the dominant theater unless Tehran chooses a sharper widening step.
- Diplomacy and mediation:
  - Direct talks remain unlikely in the baseline.
  - The more plausible de-escalatory path is mediated narrowing of attacks, not a quick comprehensive settlement.
- Economic and sanctions signaling:
  - Emergency energy-market management is likely to remain part of conflict behavior.
  - Additional legal-financial pressure on Iran's leadership network is more likely than a brand-new sanctions architecture.

- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

- Trigger: Tehran openly declares or clearly enforces a full closure of Hormuz
- Why it matters: This would cross a higher threshold than the week's ambiguity-based coercion pattern
- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

- Trigger: Hezbollah shifts from repeated attacks into dense sustained salvos and broader operational commitment
- Why it matters: This would move Lebanon from active-expanding into full-surge territory
- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

- Why it matters: This would widen the war geographically and overturn the current mechanism hierarchy

- Why it matters: This would indicate economic and escalation constraints are shaping behavior more directly
- Which scenario it supports: De-escalatory / negotiation alternative

## Fast-Fail Signals
- The forecast is wrong quickly if a formal ceasefire or direct U.S.-Iran talks are announced early in the window.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if major ship attacks, mine incidents, and Hormuz threats fall away for several consecutive days without replacement by another coercive mechanism.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if the war jumps immediately into synchronized maximum-force proxy escalation across Lebanon, Iraq, and maritime theaters.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if the U.S. or Israel visibly pause major strike operations for several days without an obvious replacement coercive track.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if decisive evidence of elite fracture or leadership collapse appears inside Iran, overturning the current resilience baseline.

## Confidence And Limits
- The biggest uncertainties are Iran's real remaining maritime-disruption capacity, the degree of control Mojtaba Khamenei and the IRGC are exercising day to day, and how far Israel is willing to push the Lebanon front.
- Evidence is still thin on the internal state of Iranian elite cohesion, the exact attribution of several shipping incidents, and the practical effect of leadership-targeting reward measures.
- Confidence labels on individual forecasts reflect evidence strength for those behaviors; scenario probabilities reflect relative path likelihoods and should not be read as certainty.
