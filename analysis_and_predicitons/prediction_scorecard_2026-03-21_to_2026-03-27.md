## Executive Summary
- The forecast's baseline was broadly right: the week stayed inside a coercive endurance war with continued U.S.-Israeli strikes, sustained Iranian maritime and energy pressure, no ground invasion, and no real diplomatic breakthrough.
- The strongest forecast hits were U.S. reinforcement and maritime-security preparation, Israel's continued high operational tempo, Iran's use of Hormuz coercive ambiguity, and the continued weakness of any clean de-escalatory path.
- The forecast also read the proxy picture well overall: Lebanon stayed active, Iraq did not become the dominant widening front, and maritime disruption mattered more than any single proxy actor narrative.
- Several claims were only partially right because the week was more contradictory than forecast: Washington mixed deadline extensions and claims of productive contacts with continued force buildup, and Israel operated under only limited diplomatic restraint.
- The biggest misses were threshold-crossing developments that were not explicitly modeled: Iran's long-range strike toward Diego Garcia, the attack that wounded U.S. troops at Prince Sultan Air Base, Israel's widening strikes on nuclear-linked sites, and the emergence of a controlled-access or toll-booth-style Hormuz regime.
- Another important miss was analytical rather than tactical: the forecast did not anticipate how clearly public U.S. success messaging would diverge from intelligence-based assessments of actual Iranian missile attrition.

## Actor-By-Actor Review
### United States
- What was forecast: Continued strikes, more reinforcements, Hormuz coalition-shaping, energy-market management, and no immediate ground invasion
- What happened: Washington kept reinforcing the theater, delayed deadlines rather than launching immediate infrastructure strikes, pushed indirect diplomacy, and continued planning around Hormuz security without a ground-entry decision
- What was missed: The forecast did not fully model repeated deadline extensions, the degree to which public claims of negotiation would dominate the week, or the intelligence gap that undercut triumphalist battle-damage messaging

### Israel
- What was forecast: Continued high-tempo strikes in Iran, sustained pressure in Lebanon, broader strategic war aims, and selective acceptance of some U.S. restraint
- What happened: Israel kept striking Iran and Lebanon, publicly rejected being folded into the talks, and widened target categories to nuclear-linked and industrial sites
- What was missed: The forecast underweighted how quickly Israel could expand into more sensitive target classes even while Washington was publicly talking diplomacy

### Iran
- What was forecast: Hormuz coercive ambiguity, regime-defiance messaging, pressure on Gulf energy and commerce, and rhetorically closed or conditional negotiation behavior
- What happened: Iran threatened Gulf infrastructure, maintained a selective-access posture in Hormuz, denied talks, issued maximal counterconditions, and kept up missile and drone pressure
- What was missed: The forecast did not explicitly anticipate the reported long-range strike toward Diego Garcia, the direct attack that wounded U.S. troops in Saudi Arabia, or the evolution from simple shipping disruption to a toll-like control model

### Hezbollah
- What was forecast: An active but not fully surged front that would keep Israel tied down in Lebanon
- What happened: The Lebanon front remained active, Israeli retaliation remained heavy, and Hezbollah-linked exchanges continued without becoming the dominant theater
- What was missed: The week was more significant for the sustained front effect than for any clearly new Hezbollah operational leap

### Hashd
- What was forecast: A latent or sporadic escalation channel rather than the main widening front
- What happened: Iraq did not become the central theater of the week
- What was missed: Very little; this remained one of the cleaner actor calls

### Ansar Allah / Houthis
- What was forecast: A structurally relevant but attribution-conditional maritime actor
- What happened: Maritime disruption stayed central without requiring a Houthi-led explanation, while by 2026-03-27 Houthi entry conditions became more visible
- What was missed: The forecast could have distinguished more clearly between current operational role and latent entry signaling

## Missed Developments
- Iran reportedly launched a long-range strike toward Diego Garcia, widening the conflict beyond the forecast's main geographic envelope
- An Iranian missile-and-drone attack on Prince Sultan Air Base in Saudi Arabia wounded U.S. service members, showing a more direct U.S.-exposure path than the forecast emphasized
- Hormuz evolved toward a controlled-access or toll-booth-style coercive regime rather than only generic ambiguity or a formal closure threat
- Israel widened strikes to nuclear-linked sites and industrial targets faster than the forecast explicitly modeled
- Reuters reporting showed a major gap between public U.S. damage claims and intelligence-confirmed missile attrition, which mattered for judging real war durability

## Why The Forecast Missed
- Over-weighted signals:
  - The assumption that coercive ambiguity in Hormuz would remain mostly below new military threshold crossings
  - The idea that indirect diplomacy would stay secondary enough not to dominate public-weekly interpretation
- Under-weighted signals:
  - The possibility that Iran would pair negotiation denial with concrete long-range or base-targeting escalation
  - The risk that Israel would widen sensitive target categories even while the U.S. was publicly discussing a ceasefire framework
  - The chance that partial-access shipping control would emerge as a durable coercive form distinct from simple threat signaling
- Timing errors:
  - The forecast was too slow to model how quickly mediated proposals and repeated U.S. deadline extensions could become a central feature of the week without producing actual de-escalation
- Actor-control errors:
  - The forecast slightly overstated how much U.S. pressure would shape Israeli target selection once diplomacy became more visible
- Propaganda traps:
  - Public U.S. claims of productive contacts and heavy Iranian degradation were not separated sharply enough from evidence of continuing battlefield capacity
- Structural misunderstandings:
  - The review suggests that Hormuz should be modeled as a spectrum of access-control regimes, not only as open, threatened, or formally closed

## Calibration Notes
- Which confidence calls were too high:
  - The implied confidence that psychological expansion would outpace operational expansion
  - The implied confidence that Israeli freedom of action would stay bounded mainly by U.S. target-restraint pressure
- Which confidence calls were too low:
  - The baseline expectation that maritime coercion would remain the conflict's central organizing feature
  - The expectation that Hashd would stay secondary rather than becoming the main widening front
- Which actor was hardest to estimate correctly:
  - The United States, because reinforcement, coercive bargaining, public optimism, intelligence reality, and no-ground-force messaging all pointed in different directions at once

## Improvements For Next Forecast
- Model Hormuz with at least three states: open passage, selective controlled passage, and formal closure
- Separate public contact claims, indirect message traffic, and observed behavioral de-escalation into different indicators rather than treating them as one diplomacy signal
- Add a threshold-crossing watchlist for long-range Iranian strike geography and direct attacks on U.S. personnel outside the core Israel-Iran exchange
- Track intelligence-validated damage assessments separately from official battlefield victory claims
- When Israel is openly outside the diplomatic track, raise the probability that target-category widening will outpace mediated restraint
