# Prediction - 2026-03-07 to 2026-03-13

## Scope
- Forecast window: 2026-03-07 to 2026-03-13
- Analysis basis: pattern analysis from the prior 7 days

## Executive Summary
- The most likely outcome is continued high-intensity but not yet decisive U.S.-Israeli military pressure on Iran, paired with sustained Iranian retaliatory fire and maritime disruption rather than rapid collapse or immediate negotiation.
- The U.S. and Israel are likely to keep the operational initiative through air and naval power, but their freedom of action will increasingly be shaped by Gulf-state defense burdens, shipping insecurity, and partner frustration.
- Iran is likely to keep retaliating in observable forms that impose regional costs without requiring full conventional parity, especially drones, missiles at reduced tempo, and pressure on maritime traffic and exposed Gulf-linked sites.
- Hezbollah is likely to remain active on a limited-to-moderate basis, enough to tie down Israeli attention and expand Lebanon costs without guaranteeing a full second war front.
- Hashd and other Iran-aligned actors remain the most plausible reserve escalation tools if Tehran seeks to widen pressure on U.S. assets quickly.
- A sharper escalation path becomes more likely if the U.S. or Israel intensifies leadership-targeting, if Gulf casualties rise materially, or if shipping protection missions produce direct U.S.-Iran naval clashes near Hormuz.
- A de-escalatory path still exists, but it would likely require indirect mediation, a pause in the most politically escalatory rhetoric, and some visible reduction in attacks on Gulf states and shipping rather than an immediate formal settlement.
- Forecast confidence is constrained by poor visibility into Iran's remaining missile inventory, unclear backchannel diplomacy, and uncertainty over whether U.S. succession rhetoric reflects actual policy or improvisation.

## Cross-Theater Forecast
- Maritime:
  - Shipping risk in and around Hormuz is likely to remain elevated.
  - The most likely maritime pattern is continued harassment, attacks, or credible threats against commercial traffic rather than an immediate formal closure of the strait.
- Air and missile campaign:
  - U.S.-Israeli air operations are likely to stay dominant.
  - Iranian missile use may remain below opening-day volume but is unlikely to disappear; substitution toward drones and opportunistic targeting is more plausible than total suppression.
- Proxy front activation:
  - Lebanon is likely to remain the most active partner front.
  - Iraq remains the most important latent escalation reserve against U.S. positions.
- Diplomacy and mediation:
  - Mediation is more likely to remain indirect and exploratory than to produce a full settlement this week.
  - Gulf pressure for restraint may increase even if public alignment against Iran continues.
- Economic and sanctions signaling:
  - The more immediate coercive tools next week are military and maritime rather than new sanctions packages.
  - If diplomacy remains frozen, sanctions rhetoric may still intensify as a supporting pressure layer rather than the main operational driver.

## Key Triggers To Watch
- Trigger: Confirmed U.S. or Israeli strike on senior succession figures or a newly selected supreme leader
- Why it matters: This would sharply raise regime-survival stakes and likely push Iran toward wider retaliation
- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

- Trigger: Public U.S. naval escort mission through Hormuz after additional shipping attacks
- Why it matters: Escort operations increase the chance of direct tactical U.S.-Iran clashes
- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

- Trigger: Hezbollah shifts from controlled fire to dense sustained salvos or broader cross-border operations
- Why it matters: This would turn Lebanon from a secondary pressure front into a major war theater
- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

- Trigger: Clear Omani, E3, or Gulf-mediated contact acknowledged by both sides
- Why it matters: This would indicate diplomacy has regained some operational relevance
- Which scenario it supports: De-escalatory / negotiation alternative

- Trigger: Visible drop in attacks on Gulf infrastructure and shipping without compensating escalation elsewhere
- Why it matters: This would suggest Iran is preserving bargaining space and coalition strain is having an effect
- Which scenario it supports: De-escalatory / negotiation alternative

- Trigger: Verified activation of Hashd against U.S. bases
- Why it matters: This would widen the war's geography and increase U.S. retaliation pressure
- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

## Fast-Fail Signals
- The forecast is wrong quickly if formal ceasefire or direct talks are announced early in the window.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if the U.S. or Israel visibly pause major strike operations for several consecutive days without replacement by another coercive channel.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if Iran's retaliation collapses far more sharply than expected and no proxy or maritime substitution appears.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if Hezbollah remains fully quiet despite continued Israeli operations against Lebanon-linked targets.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if the conflict jumps straight to a qualitatively new theater, such as large coordinated militia attacks across Iraq-Syria or openly acknowledged territorial seizure attempts inside Iran.

## Confidence And Limits
- The biggest uncertainties are Iran's remaining launch capacity, the real state of internal succession politics, and whether Gulf partner strain is strong enough to alter U.S. choices in the next few days rather than later.
- Evidence is thin on the true operational status of diplomatic backchannels and on how much control Tehran is exerting over each aligned actor in real time.
- Confidence labels on individual forecast lines reflect evidence strength for those behaviors; scenario probabilities reflect comparative path likelihoods and should not be read as certainty.
