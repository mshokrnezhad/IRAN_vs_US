# Prediction - 2026-03-21 to 2026-03-27

## Scope
- Forecast window: 2026-03-21 to 2026-03-27
- Analysis basis: pattern analysis from the prior 7 days

## Executive Summary
- The most likely outcome is continued coercive endurance warfare with high U.S.-Israeli strike activity, persistent Iranian shipping and energy coercion, and no real diplomatic breakthrough.
- The strongest near-term signal remains U.S. operational preparation rather than U.S. rhetorical optimism; deployments, funding, and contingency planning point to war continuation even if public messaging suggests nearing objectives.
- U.S.-Israeli alignment is likely to hold militarily while target-priority and end-state divergence becomes more visible, especially around energy infrastructure and the acceptable scope of escalation.
- Iran is more likely to keep using ambiguous, economically painful pressure on shipping, Gulf infrastructure, and regional confidence than to jump immediately to a clearly declared total closure of Hormuz.
- Domestic-political friction inside the United States is now a forecast variable: legitimacy disputes are unlikely to stop operations this week, but they can complicate escalation durability and messaging discipline.
- Israel is likely to maintain offensive tempo in Iran and keep the Lebanon front active, but with selective U.S. pressure to avoid the most globally disruptive target choices.
- Hezbollah, Iraqi partner forces, and Houthis remain relevant escalation channels, but the baseline still favors uneven, theater-specific pressure rather than synchronized maximum-force activation across all proxy fronts.
- The biggest escalatory risks are a direct U.S.-Iran maritime clash, an actual operational coalition entering Hormuz security missions, or a sharper shift from regional threats to more clearly executed attacks on Gulf energy hubs or U.S. facilities.

## Cross-Theater Forecast
- Maritime:
  - Shipping risk around Hormuz is likely to stay elevated.
  - The most likely pattern is continuing coercive ambiguity, escort preparation, and selective disruption rather than a clean binary of open versus closed.
- Air and missile campaign:
  - U.S.-Israeli strikes are likely to continue at high tempo.
  - Iran is more likely to keep mixing missiles, drones, and energy-pressure tactics than to rely only on early-war-style mass salvos.
- Proxy front activation:
  - Lebanon is likely to remain the most operationally active partner front.
  - Iraq remains more likely to produce spillover or opportunistic attacks than to become the main theater unless maritime events trigger wider retaliation.
- Diplomacy and mediation:
  - Direct U.S.-Iran diplomacy remains unlikely in the baseline.
  - The most realistic de-escalatory path is mediated narrowing of targets and tempo, not a comprehensive settlement.
- Economic and sanctions signaling:
  - More emergency oil-market management is likely.
  - The U.S. is also likely to continue converting battlefield alignment into legal and diplomatic pressure, including anti-IRGC or anti-Hezbollah coordination.

## Key Triggers To Watch
- Trigger: A direct U.S.-Iran naval clash tied to escort, mine-clearing, interdiction, or tanker defense
- Why it matters: This would move the war into a more openly bilateral maritime phase
- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

- Trigger: One or more allied governments publicly commit naval assets to a Hormuz security mission
- Why it matters: This would show coalition formation finally catching up to coalition solicitation
- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

- Trigger: Repeated strikes on major Gulf energy sites, ports, or airports with visible market shock
- Why it matters: This would show Iran or Iran-linked pressure crossing from signaling to a more punishing energy-war lane
- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

- Trigger: Israel visibly resumes attacks on Iranian gas infrastructure despite U.S. objections
- Why it matters: This would show war-aim divergence becoming less manageable
- Which scenario it supports: Escalatory alternative

- Trigger: Mutually acknowledged mediator contact coincides with several quieter days in shipping and energy targeting
- Why it matters: This would be stronger evidence of real de-escalation than generalized calls for restraint
- Which scenario it supports: De-escalatory / negotiation alternative

- Trigger: U.S. deployments continue, but public and private messaging shifts toward narrower target sets and capped objectives
- Why it matters: This would indicate Washington is trying to stabilize rather than widen the campaign
- Which scenario it supports: De-escalatory / negotiation alternative

## Fast-Fail Signals
- The forecast is wrong quickly if a formal ceasefire, direct U.S.-Iran talks, or a mutually acknowledged negotiating framework emerges early in the window.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if the U.S. enters an overt ground-control or site-seizure phase around Iranian territory or infrastructure.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if shipping and Gulf-energy threats drop sharply for several consecutive days without being replaced by another major coercive mechanism.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if Hezbollah, Iraqi militias, maritime actors, and Gulf-targeting behavior all surge together into a clearly synchronized maximum-force escalation.
- The forecast is wrong quickly if domestic U.S. political fracture produces immediate binding operational limits that materially slow the campaign inside the week.

## Confidence And Limits
- The biggest uncertainties are the real threshold for U.S. operational expansion, the degree of allied willingness to participate in Hormuz security, and how far Israel will test U.S. tolerance on target selection.
- Evidence remains thin on the precise operational readiness of Iraqi partner forces and Houthis in this specific week, even though both remain structurally relevant.
- Iranian threat messaging is broader than the fully verified action set, so some signals may be more psychological or propagandistic than operational.
- Confidence labels describe the strength of evidence for specific forecasted behaviors; scenario probabilities describe relative path likelihood and are not certainty claims.
