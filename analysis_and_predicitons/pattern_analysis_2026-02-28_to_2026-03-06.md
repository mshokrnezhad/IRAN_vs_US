## Executive Summary
- The week marked a decisive threshold crossing from crisis signaling into openly declared, sustained U.S.-Israeli interstate war against Iran, beginning on `2026-02-28` and continuing without a clear near-term end state.
- The United States and Israel showed a repeated pattern of expanding strike scope over time: initial attacks on Iranian military and missile targets gave way to deeper inland strikes, naval warfare, wider Lebanon operations, and increasingly explicit discussion of shaping Iran's leadership outcome.
- Iran retaliated immediately and persistently, but the pattern shifted from high-intensity missile salvos toward a mixed missile-drone-maritime pressure campaign as U.S. officials claimed Iranian missile output was declining.
- Hezbollah moved from ambiguous or possibly indirect involvement to more open fire from Lebanon, but its role still appeared bounded relative to the scale of the main Iran-U.S.-Israel theater.
- Gulf states became both part of the battlefield and a growing constraint on the U.S. campaign: Iranian retaliation hit or threatened Gulf territory, air defenses were strained, and allied complaints about inadequate warning and protection became a visible political signal by the end of the week.
- Diplomacy did not disappear, but it lost operational weight. Omani channels and European calls for restraint persisted while both Washington and Tehran publicly hardened their positions, culminating in Trump's `unconditional surrender` demand on `2026-03-06`.
- The nuclear file became more tightly fused with battlefield escalation, especially after reporting that Natanz had been hit, reinforcing the longstanding fundamental pattern that nuclear crisis and regional war risk move together.
- The week's most important uncertainty was whether coalition strain and Iranian attrition would slow escalation, or whether leadership-targeting rhetoric, maritime threats, and possible new fronts would drive a broader multi-theater surge next.

## Weekly Baseline
The week's overall state was high-intensity interstate war with multi-front regional spillover. The baseline was not simple escalation in a straight line; it was a sustained coercive campaign in which the U.S. and Israel retained the initiative in the air and at sea, while Iran kept enough retaliatory capacity to threaten Israel, Gulf infrastructure, shipping, and U.S. positions indirectly and directly. The system looked less like a short punitive strike cycle and more like an open-ended campaign with simultaneous military attrition, succession pressure inside Iran, and weakening diplomatic traction.

## Actor Behavior Analysis
### United States
- Objectives observed:
  - Destroy Iranian missile, naval, and nuclear-linked capability.
  - Protect Israel and U.S. regional force posture while degrading Iran's capacity to retaliate.
  - Increase pressure on the Iranian regime and, by the end of the week, publicly shape debate over Iran's post-Khamenei leadership.
  - Maintain domestic political room for continued military action.
- Actions taken:
  - Entered the war directly alongside Israel on `2026-02-28`.
  - Sustained strikes across Iran for seven straight days, with official target totals rising from more than 1,000 to nearly 2,000.
  - Signaled a campaign expected to last weeks, not days.
  - Expanded toward deeper inland strikes and maritime warfare, including sinking an Iranian warship and claiming large Iranian naval losses.
  - Secured Senate and House backing against war-powers limits.
  - Shifted public messaging from military degradation toward explicit demands for surrender and rhetoric about acceptable Iranian leadership outcomes.
- Constraints:
  - Iranian retaliation killed six U.S. service members and exposed regional bases and diplomatic sites.
  - Gulf allies reported inadequate warning, insufficient protection, and depleting interceptor stocks.
  - Public U.S. messaging showed some inconsistency on war aims and entry rationale.
  - Even amid public claims of battlefield success, U.S. officials acknowledged problems countering cheap Iranian drone waves.
- Pattern assessment:
  - The clearest U.S. pattern was escalation through operational deepening plus political hardening. Behavior mattered more than rhetoric: Washington repeatedly widened the campaign, lengthened the implied timeline, and resisted institutional limits at home.
  - A second pattern was objective drift risk. Official statements still framed the mission around capability destruction, but repeated leadership-selection comments and surrender demands suggested pressure was extending beyond a narrow military objective set.
  - A third pattern was hidden burden accumulation. Operational initiative remained with Washington, but allied strain and drone-defense gaps showed that the campaign was not cost-free or frictionless.

### Israel
- Objectives observed:
  - Destroy Iranian military, missile, air-defense, and nuclear-linked targets.
  - Reduce the long-term threat of Iranian regional encirclement.
  - Degrade Hezbollah and other Iran-aligned fronts before they could fully saturate the conflict.
  - Influence the post-Khamenei strategic environment inside Iran.
- Actions taken:
  - Launched the war jointly with the U.S. under `Operation Lion's Roar`.
  - Claimed growing control of Iranian airspace and continued strikes on Tehran, Qom, missile sites, and nuclear-linked facilities.
  - Broadened strikes in Lebanon after Hezbollah fire resumed.
  - Used increasingly explicit leadership-targeting rhetoric, including suggestions that Iran's next supreme leader could become a target.
  - Indicated readiness for a campaign measured in weeks while ruling out a likely ground invasion of Iran.
- Constraints:
  - Continued Iranian missile fire into Israel, though interception rates appeared to remain high.
  - Hezbollah's entry increased pressure on the northern front.
  - Israel still depended on U.S. military participation and broader regional defense support.
  - Israeli objectives may not have been perfectly aligned with Washington's officially narrower framing.
- Pattern assessment:
  - Israel showed a repeated pattern of offensive tempo maintenance and theater expansion under the cover of U.S. partnership.
  - The week suggested Israel was pushing hardest on the "degrade now while initiative is ours" logic: air, nuclear-linked, leadership, and Lebanese targets were all treated as part of one campaign.
  - Israel's behavior also fit the long-term fundamental pattern that shadow-war logic had broken down; this week it acted as if direct sustained war with Iran had become acceptable and manageable.

### Iran
- Objectives observed:
  - Restore deterrence after the opening strikes and Khamenei's death.
  - Impose regional costs on the U.S., Israel, and Gulf states hosting U.S. forces.
  - Preserve regime continuity during succession shock.
  - Avoid public surrender while keeping some possibility of later diplomacy alive through indirect channels.
- Actions taken:
  - Retaliated immediately on `2026-02-28` against Israel and multiple Gulf states.
  - Continued missile barrages into Israel and attacks or claimed attacks against Gulf bases, tankers, energy infrastructure, embassies, and shipping lanes.
  - Confirmed Khamenei's death and installed a temporary leadership council.
  - Rejected near-term negotiations publicly, then by `2026-03-06` faced a U.S. demand for unconditional surrender.
  - Used official messaging to combine defiance, victimization narratives, and selective signals of conditional openness through Oman early in the week.
- Constraints:
  - Sustained U.S.-Israeli air and naval superiority.
  - Severe attrition against missile launchers, air defenses, leadership, and naval assets was repeatedly claimed by the U.S. and Israel.
  - Russia and China were rhetorically sympathetic but did not materially alter the battlefield.
  - Leadership succession and internal security pressures reduced Tehran's room for flexible diplomacy.
- Pattern assessment:
  - Iran's core pattern was persistent retaliation under attrition. Tehran did not fold, but its retaliation appeared to shift toward lower-cost, more distributed, and more deniable instruments as the week went on.
  - The behavior matched long-term fundamentals: use layered deterrence through missiles, drones, maritime threats, and partner networks rather than rely on one decisive conventional counterstrike.
  - Iran also displayed a familiar divergence between rhetoric and bargaining posture. Public rejection of talks was stronger than the limited early-week signs that backchannels might still exist.

### Hezbollah
- Role this week:
  - Transitioned from possible or ambiguous involvement at the start of the week to overt fire from Lebanon by `2026-03-02`, followed by major Israeli retaliation and later signs of deeper confrontation in Lebanon.
- Pattern assessment:
  - Hezbollah behaved like an activated but still somewhat bounded deterrent arm rather than the main theater driver.
  - Its entry reinforced the historic pattern described in the fundamentals: Hezbollah remains Iran's most capable regional partner and the most credible mechanism for pressuring Israel from the northern front.
  - At the same time, the scale of its observed activity did not yet indicate an all-out maximum-force entry comparable to a full independent war effort.

### Iraqi Partner Forces
- Role this week:
  - No clearly confirmed major Iraqi militia operational role dominated the week's reporting, though AP and the fundamentals both framed Iraqi partner forces as part of the wider Iranian pressure architecture and regional risk environment.
- Pattern assessment:
  - The key pattern was latent capability rather than visible employment. Iraqi partner forces remained relevant as a standing escalation option against U.S. positions, but they were not the central observed driver in this seven-day window.
  - That absence matters analytically because it suggests Iran did not fully synchronize every available partner front at once, consistent with the guardrail that Iranian-aligned actors should not be assumed to move in perfect lockstep.

### Ansar Allah / Houthis
- Role this week:
  - No distinct Houthi operational event dominated the daily briefs, but maritime disruption behavior in Gulf and Hormuz-adjacent waters remained consistent with the broader pattern of Iran-aligned pressure on shipping and sea lanes.
- Pattern assessment:
  - The week's observable maritime pressure fit the long-term fundamental role of the Houthis and other Iran-aligned actors in widening conflict costs through shipping disruption, even when the main headlines were elsewhere.
  - As with Iraqi groups, the notable point was strategic relevance without clear evidence of peak activation in this specific week.

## Cross-Actor Interaction Patterns
- The U.S.-Israeli opening strikes immediately triggered an Iranian retaliation pattern that widened the battlefield beyond Israel to Gulf bases, energy infrastructure, shipping, and diplomatic sites, turning third-party states into both participants and constraints.
- Israeli and U.S. pressure on Iran's air defenses, missile launchers, and leadership produced an adaptive Iranian response: fewer visible missile salvos were paired with persistent drone and maritime harassment, suggesting substitution rather than cessation.
- Hezbollah's more open fire from Lebanon led Israel to widen its Lebanon campaign, demonstrating a direct escalation loop between proxy activation and secondary-theater bombardment.
- Gulf vulnerability fed back into coalition politics. Iranian attacks did not break the U.S.-Israel campaign, but they did create a restraint loop by increasing allied complaints and resource strain.
- Washington's increasingly explicit talk about Iranian leadership and surrender likely narrowed Tehran's public space for negotiation, even as battlefield attrition might have otherwise made backchannel de-escalation more attractive.
