## Executive Summary
- The week marked a transition from opening-war shock into coercive endurance, with the center of gravity shifting toward shipping, oil flows, leadership pressure, and regime resilience rather than only raw strike counts.
- Mojtaba Khamenei's formal elevation on `2026-03-08` resolved the immediate succession vacuum faster than the prior forecast allowed and gave Iran a harder, more IRGC-aligned public command structure.
- The United States and Israel maintained operational initiative, but U.S. behavior broadened beyond strike continuation into maritime protection, leadership-targeting pressure, and active energy-market management.
- Iran's repeated pattern was not outright closure of the Strait of Hormuz, but calibrated coercive ambiguity: threaten oil flows, mine or menace shipping, raise market costs, and preserve room to deny full closure.
- Israeli behavior shifted further toward explicit regime-collapse and leadership-delegitimization framing while keeping military pressure high in Iran and expanding operations in Lebanon.
- Hezbollah moved from an active-bounded role toward an active-expanding one, while Hashd remained relevant but did not become the main widening mechanism during the week.
- The strongest corrective to propaganda-heavy collapse narratives was behavioral evidence: U.S. intelligence reportedly judged the Iranian state durable, and senior Iranian figures appeared publicly despite continuing strikes.
- Outside powers still sought restraint, but the more operationally meaningful external response was oil and shipping stabilization, not a viable ceasefire process.

## Weekly Baseline
The week's overall state was sustained high-intensity interstate war under an endurance logic. Relative to the prior week, the key change was not a fresh opening threshold but consolidation: Iran replaced leadership uncertainty with a hardline succession outcome, the U.S. and Israel kept the air and naval initiative, and the conflict's most important leverage points became Hormuz, merchant shipping, Gulf infrastructure, Lebanon escalation, and political warfare over regime durability. The system looked less like a short attritional sprint and more like a coercive contest over who could absorb cost, shape the narrative of legitimacy, and manage regional economic shock.

## Actor Behavior Analysis
### United States
- Objectives observed:
  - Sustain the attritional air and naval campaign against Iran.
  - Prevent a successful Iranian disruption of oil flows and merchant shipping.
  - Keep political and intelligence pressure on Iran's top leadership, especially after Mojtaba Khamenei's elevation.
  - Support Israel while limiting economic blowback severe enough to fracture the coalition or global markets.
- Actions taken:
  - Publicly tied war termination to joint U.S.-Israeli decision-making.
  - Claimed destruction of Iranian minelayers and a continuing high target count inside Iran.
  - Threatened far harsher retaliation if Iran did not remove mines or if oil flows through Hormuz were obstructed.
  - Backed record-scale strategic oil release planning and eased some Russian-oil restrictions to stabilize supply.
  - Expanded leadership pressure by offering rewards for information on Mojtaba Khamenei and other IRGC-linked figures.
  - Reportedly urged Israel to stop striking some Iranian energy sites, showing selective restraint inside an otherwise escalatory campaign.
- Constraints:
  - Gulf partners remained exposed and frustrated by air-defense strain and shipping insecurity.
  - Oil-market disruption imposed economic pressure that Washington had to manage in real time.
  - U.S. intelligence reporting reportedly did not support imminent regime-collapse claims, limiting the credibility of maximalist rhetoric.
  - Direct maritime confrontation risk around Hormuz remained high if escort or mine-clearing operations expanded.
- Pattern assessment:
  - The clearest U.S. pattern was operational continuity plus functional broadening. Washington did not just keep striking; it added three reinforcing tracks: shipping defense, leadership isolation, and energy stabilization.
  - A second pattern was rhetoric-behavior divergence. Public messaging often sounded closer to victory or regime-collapse logic than the available evidence justified, but actual policy behavior showed more caution, especially around oil shock and Israeli target selection.
  - A third pattern was that U.S. coercion increasingly blended military, intelligence-financial, and market-governance tools rather than relying on bombardment alone.

### Israel
- Objectives observed:
  - Preserve offensive momentum against Iranian military and nuclear-linked targets.
  - Use the succession moment to deepen pressure on the new Iranian leadership.
  - Keep Hezbollah from turning Lebanon into a fully separate high-intensity war on terms favorable to Iran.
  - Push political framing toward delegitimizing Mojtaba Khamenei and weakening regime confidence.
- Actions taken:
  - Continued high-tempo strikes inside Iran.
  - Repeatedly framed war termination as something Israel and the U.S. would decide together.
  - Expanded operations in Lebanon after Hezbollah barrages.
  - Publicly threatened to pursue hostile Iranian successors and later argued Israeli attacks were creating conditions for regime collapse.
  - Maintained pressure across multiple target classes rather than narrowing to a single theater.
- Constraints:
  - Israel still faced continued Iranian retaliatory capability and an active Lebanon front.
  - Its campaign remained strategically intertwined with U.S. military support and U.S. management of wider economic fallout.
  - A full second war in Lebanon would still impose major costs and dilute focus from Iran.
- Pattern assessment:
  - Israel showed a repeated pattern of keeping tempo high while widening the political meaning of the campaign.
  - Compared with the prior week, Israeli rhetoric moved more openly from threat reduction toward regime-collapse conditions, though battlefield behavior still centered on sustained coercive attrition rather than immediate decapitation success.
  - Lebanon's role also shifted: Hezbollah was no longer just a background secondary front, and Israeli behavior increasingly treated Lebanese escalation as part of one integrated campaign.

### Iran
- Objectives observed:
  - Preserve regime continuity after Ali Khamenei's death and under Mojtaba Khamenei's succession.
  - Impose economic, maritime, and regional costs on the U.S., Israel, and Gulf states.
  - Avoid public capitulation while retaining some coercive ambiguity over the scale of future escalation.
  - Demonstrate that the state remained governable and visible despite bombardment.
- Actions taken:
  - Installed Mojtaba Khamenei as supreme leader and later used his first public wartime statement to define a harder line.
  - Threatened oil flows, banks, merchant shipping, and U.S.-linked regional infrastructure.
  - Kept using missiles, drones, shipping pressure, and Gulf-targeting behavior rather than relying on one instrument alone.
  - Sent mixed but not contradictory Hormuz signals: Mojtaba favored continued leverage, while Iran's U.N. envoy denied an outright closure decision.
  - Threatened internal dissent harshly while staging public appearances by senior officials and rally participation to display continuity.
- Constraints:
  - U.S.-Israeli air and naval superiority continued to impose heavy attrition and uncertainty.
  - Iran remained isolated strategically, with Russia and China not altering battlefield dynamics in a material way.
  - A full formal closure of Hormuz risked inviting even larger U.S. retaliation and wider international entry.
  - Tehran still had to balance deterrent signaling with the need to avoid proving U.S.-Israeli regime-collapse claims correct.
- Pattern assessment:
  - Iran's main pattern was coercive ambiguity under a resilience narrative. It repeatedly threatened maximal disruption but usually behaved one step short of the most escalatory formal threshold.
  - Mojtaba's succession mattered because it hardened the public line quickly, but it did not translate into immediate abandonment of calibrated signaling. The regime combined hardline rhetoric with selective deniability and ambiguity.
  - The week also reinforced a long-term structural pattern: when Iran is under direct attritional pressure, it seeks leverage through distributed costs across shipping, oil, partner fronts, and political symbolism rather than through one decisive conventional answer.

### Hezbollah
- Role this week:
  - Hezbollah kept the northern front active and helped pull Lebanon from an active-bounded theater toward an active-expanding one, especially after barrages on `2026-03-12` triggered plans for broader Israeli operations.
- Pattern assessment:
  - Relative to the prior week, Hezbollah looked hotter and more consequential.
  - Even so, the observed pattern still fell short of an unmistakable full-surge independent war effort. Hezbollah remained a meaningful but still subordinated escalation arm inside the wider Iran-Israel-U.S. conflict.

### Hashd
- Role this week:
  - Iraq mattered as a spillover theater, including reported attacks affecting Irbil, but no clearly confirmed Iraqi partner-force campaign became the central widening mechanism.
- Pattern assessment:
  - The pattern was latent-to-sporadic relevance rather than dominant activation.
  - This matters analytically because it confirmed a lesson from the previous scorecard: wider pressure on U.S. interests can occur without Iraqi militias becoming the main instrument.

### Ansar Allah / Houthis
- Role this week:
  - No single Houthi-attributed event dominated the daily briefs, but the week kept reinforcing the attractiveness of maritime disruption and sea-lane pressure as an Iran-aligned coercive tool.
- Pattern assessment:
  - The strategic function associated with the Houthis remained highly relevant even when specific incident attribution stayed mixed.
  - The observable pattern was supportive pressure on shipping and regional economic vulnerability rather than a separate headline theater overtaking Hormuz.

## Cross-Actor Interaction Patterns
- Mojtaba Khamenei's selection quickly triggered a new interaction loop: U.S. and Israeli rhetoric moved from succession speculation to delegitimization and leadership targeting, while Iran answered with harder public doctrine and resilience theater.
- Iranian maritime threats and actual shipping disruption pushed the United States and partner institutions toward mine-clearing, escort discussion, reserve releases, and sanctions-waiver-style energy management, showing how Iranian coercion influenced not just military posture but market policy.
- Hezbollah barrages produced a visible Israeli widening response in Lebanon, reinforcing an escalation loop in which even limited proxy activation could generate major secondary-theater retaliation.
- U.S. intelligence reporting that Iran's regime was not near collapse interacted awkwardly with maximalist public rhetoric, leading Washington to rely more on leadership pressure, reward notices, and symbolic isolation rather than on a credible claim of imminent state failure.
- Gulf exposure remained a restraint loop rather than a war-stopping one. Partner vulnerability did not end the campaign, but it helped shape U.S. caution on energy targets and elevated shipping defense and oil stabilization to first-order issues.

## Information Gaps
- The true extent of Iranian remaining mine, drone, missile, and shipping-disruption capacity remains unclear.
- Many battlefield damage claims by the U.S. and Israel are still stronger than the independently verified evidence available in the daily briefs.
- The line between Tehran-directed action and looser partner or copycat behavior remains murky in several maritime and regional incidents.
- Mojtaba Khamenei's exact physical condition and day-to-day operational role remain uncertain despite formal succession clarity.
- Internal Iranian elite cohesion is still hard to measure from outside reporting, even with visible public appearances.
- The practical enforcement effect of the U.S. reward program on elite behavior, intelligence collection, or battlefield decisions is unknown.
